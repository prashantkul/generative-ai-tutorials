Generative Adversarial Networks

Two networks, locked in a game — the forger learns to create, the detective learns to detect, and both get better.

By Prashant Kulkarni · with help from Claude OpusCC BY-NC 4.0
1 The core idea

Forger vs detective

A GAN trains a generative model by pitting it against an adversary. The generator never sees the real data directly — it only receives a learning signal that passes through the discriminator, telling it how to improve its forgeries.

2 The objective

The minimax game

$$\min_G \max_D \; V(D, G) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_\text{data}}[\log D(x)] + \mathbb{E}_{z \sim p_z}[\log(1 - D(G(z)))]$$

D wants to maximize: assign $D(x) \to 1$ for real data and $D(G(z)) \to 0$ for fakes. G wants to minimize: make $D(G(z)) \to 1$ (fool the discriminator). At the Nash equilibrium, $p_G = p_\text{data}$ and $D^*(x) = \frac{1}{2}$ everywhere — the discriminator can no longer tell them apart.

In practice: the non-saturating loss

The original G loss $\log(1 - D(G(z)))$ saturates when D is confident (gradients vanish). The standard workaround: G maximizes $\log D(G(z))$ instead. Same equilibrium, but stronger gradients early in training when G is poor and D is easily confident.

3 Training dynamics

Why GANs are hard to train

Unlike the stable MSE objectives of autoencoders and diffusion models, GAN training is a two-player game — and games don't converge as reliably as optimizations. Three failure modes dominate:

  • Non-convergence. Instead of converging to the Nash equilibrium, the two networks oscillate — D gets better, then G adapts, then D re-adapts, cycling without settling. Gradient descent on minimax objectives does not guarantee convergence.
  • Mode collapse (§4): G learns to produce only a few outputs that fool D, ignoring most of the data distribution. The generator discovers one "trick" that works and repeats it endlessly.
  • Vanishing gradients. If D becomes too good too fast, it outputs near-zero probabilities for all fakes, and G's gradient signal vanishes — it can't learn anything because the loss landscape is flat.

The standard mitigation: alternate updates carefully. Train D for $k$ steps, then G for 1 step. Keep D slightly ahead of G but not so far ahead that gradients vanish. This balance is fragile and the main reason GANs have a reputation for finicky training.

4 Mode collapse

When the generator takes shortcuts

Mode collapse is the signature GAN failure: the generator finds a small set of outputs that consistently fool the discriminator and produces only those, ignoring the diversity of the real data. On MNIST, a collapsed GAN might produce perfect 7s and nothing else — all ten digit classes are in the data, but G has discovered that one class is enough to minimize its loss.

"The generator doesn't want to model the whole distribution. It wants to find the one point that maximally confuses the discriminator — and camp there."

Mode collapse happens because the minimax objective doesn't explicitly penalize lack of diversity. G is rewarded for quality (fooling D), not for coverage (matching the full data distribution). The discriminator can eventually catch on and start rejecting the repeated outputs, but by then G often just switches to a different collapse — cycling between a few modes without ever covering all of them.

5 Wasserstein GAN

A better distance metric

The vanilla GAN effectively minimizes the Jensen-Shannon divergence between $p_\text{data}$ and $p_G$. When the two distributions don't overlap (which is common early in training, especially in high dimensions), JS divergence is constant and uninformative — the gradient signal is zero.

The Wasserstein GAN (Arjovsky et al., 2017) replaces the JS divergence with the Earth Mover's Distance (Wasserstein-1 distance), which provides meaningful gradients even when the distributions don't overlap:

$$\min_G \max_{D \in \text{1-Lipschitz}} \; \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_\text{data}}[D(x)] - \mathbb{E}_{z \sim p_z}[D(G(z))]$$

Key changes from vanilla GAN:

  • The discriminator (now called critic) outputs a raw score, not a probability — no sigmoid at the end.
  • The critic must be Lipschitz-constrained. The original WGAN used weight clipping; WGAN-GP (Gulrajani et al., 2017) replaced this with a gradient penalty: $\lambda \mathbb{E}_{\hat{x}}[(\|\nabla_{\hat{x}} D(\hat{x})\|_2 - 1)^2]$, which is more stable.
  • The Wasserstein loss correlates with sample quality — unlike the vanilla GAN loss, which oscillates meaninglessly. You can actually use the loss curve to monitor training.
6 Architecture

DCGAN — making it work with convolutions

The Deep Convolutional GAN (Radford et al., 2015) established the recipe that made GANs practical for images: replace fully-connected layers with convolutional layers, use batch normalization, and follow a specific set of architectural guidelines that stabilize training.

7 StyleGAN

Progressive training and style control

Progressive GAN (Karras et al., 2017) introduced the idea of growing the network during training: start generating 4×4 images, then add layers to produce 8×8, 16×16, and so on up to 1024×1024. Each resolution stage is trained until stable before the next is added. This dramatically stabilized high-resolution GAN training.

StyleGAN (Karras et al., 2019) built on this with a mapping network that transforms noise $z$ into an intermediate latent $w$ via 8 fully-connected layers, then injects $w$ into each generator layer via adaptive instance normalization (AdaIN). The $w$ space has much better properties than the original $z$ space — interpolation is smoother, attribute control is more disentangled, and individual layers control different aspects of the output (coarse layers control pose and shape; fine layers control color and texture).

StyleGAN2 and StyleGAN3 refined the approach further with improved training stability, artifact reduction, and alias-free generation. These models remain the state of the art for high-quality face synthesis.

8 Conditional GANs

Generating what you ask for

A vanilla GAN generates random samples from the data distribution — you can't control what it generates. A conditional GAN (cGAN) adds a condition $y$ (class label, text, image) to both networks:

$$\min_G \max_D \; \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_\text{data}}[\log D(x, y)] + \mathbb{E}_{z \sim p_z}[\log(1 - D(G(z, y), y))]$$

Both G and D receive the condition $y$. G must produce outputs consistent with $y$; D must verify not just realism but also condition-consistency. On MNIST, conditioning on the digit class lets you generate a specific digit on demand; on CelebA, conditioning on attributes lets you generate "smiling woman with glasses."

Pix2pix (image-to-image translation), CycleGAN (unpaired translation), and SPADE (semantic-layout-to-image) are all conditional GAN variants with paired or unpaired training data.

9 Evaluation

How do you measure generation quality?

Unlike models with explicit likelihoods (VAEs, autoregressive models), GANs don't give you a number for "how well does this model fit the data?" Two metrics dominate:

  • Fréchet Inception Distance (FID): embed real and generated images through a pretrained Inception network, model each set's embeddings as a Gaussian, and compute the Fréchet distance between the two Gaussians. Lower FID = better. FID captures both quality and diversity — a model that produces perfect but identical images will have high FID because its distribution is too narrow.
  • Inception Score (IS): measures two things via the Inception network — each generated image should be classifiable with high confidence (quality), and the marginal class distribution across all generated images should be uniform (diversity). Higher IS = better. Less reliable than FID; largely supplanted by it.
FID caveats

FID depends heavily on the sample size (need ~50K samples for stable estimates), the reference dataset, and the Inception model's biases. It also measures feature-level statistics, not perceptual quality directly. A low FID doesn't guarantee images look good to humans — but a high FID almost always means they don't.

10 Landscape

GANs vs diffusion vs VAEs

GAN

Fast generation (one forward pass). Sharp outputs. Adversarial training is fragile. Mode collapse risk. No likelihood. Hard to scale beyond images without heavy engineering.

Diffusion

High quality + diversity. Stable training (just MSE). Slow generation (many steps). Scales well. Has largely replaced GANs for image generation since 2022.

VAE

Stable training. Tractable likelihood. Blurry outputs (KL smoothing). Fast generation. Great for latent space learning. Often used as a component (Stable Diffusion's encoder).

In 2024–25, GANs are no longer the dominant paradigm for image generation — diffusion models and flow matching have largely taken over. But GANs remain important for real-time generation (one forward pass vs. 20+ denoising steps), super-resolution, style transfer, and anywhere inference speed matters more than sample diversity. Understanding GANs is also essential for understanding adversarial training, a concept that recurs in RLHF, data augmentation, and robustness research.

11 Field notes

Training GANs on 2D and MNIST

Source

Experiments from the Module 05 notebook. The notebook trains a simple GAN on a 2D ring-of-Gaussians distribution, a DCGAN on MNIST, a WGAN-GP on MNIST, and a conditional GAN on MNIST.

2D ring mixture: watching convergence

The notebook trains a simple 2-layer generator and discriminator on a mixture of 8 Gaussians arranged in a ring. Snapshots at different training steps show the generator's output points gradually moving from a random blob to eight tight clusters matching the target distribution. This is the clearest possible visualization of what GAN training does — and it also shows mode collapse when it happens (the generator covers only 5 of 8 modes in some runs).

DCGAN on MNIST: loss curves

The characteristic GAN loss curve looks nothing like the smooth descent of a diffusion model. Both G and D losses oscillate — when D gets better, G's loss rises; when G adapts, D's loss rises. The losses do not converge to zero; they settle into a noisy equilibrium. This is normal and expected. A smoothed (rolling average) version of the losses is more informative than the raw values.

WGAN-GP stability

Side-by-side comparison of vanilla GAN vs WGAN-GP shows the difference clearly: the vanilla GAN's loss oscillates wildly and sample quality is inconsistent across runs. The WGAN-GP's critic loss (Wasserstein estimate) decreases smoothly and actually correlates with sample quality — you can watch the loss go down and the generated digits get better simultaneously. This is the main practical advantage of the Wasserstein formulation.

Conditional generation

The conditional GAN generates specific digits on demand. Inputting the class label "7" produces 7s; "3" produces 3s. Interpolating in the latent space while holding the class label fixed morphs the style (stroke width, slant, size) while keeping the identity (digit class) constant — demonstrating that the generator has learned to disentangle content from style.

13 Closing

Takeaways & exercises

The six things to remember

  1. GANs are a two-player game — the generator learns to fool the discriminator, and the discriminator learns to detect fakes. Neither sees the other's data directly; they communicate only through gradients.
  2. Mode collapse is the signature failure mode — the generator finds shortcuts that fool the discriminator without covering the full data distribution.
  3. WGAN replaces JS divergence with Wasserstein distance, providing meaningful gradients even when distributions don't overlap, and a loss curve that actually correlates with sample quality.
  4. DCGAN established the convolutional recipe — transposed convolutions for upsampling, batch norm, and specific architectural guidelines that made image GANs practical.
  5. StyleGAN separated content from style via a mapping network and adaptive normalization, producing the highest-quality face synthesis to date.
  6. GANs are fast at inference (one forward pass) but have largely been replaced by diffusion models for quality and diversity. They remain relevant for real-time applications and adversarial training concepts.
